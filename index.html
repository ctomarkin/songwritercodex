The Fight This Book Settles

In May 2026, the New York Times named the thirty greatest living American songwriters. More than 250 music professionals voted — and then five critics overruled them and picked the names themselves. The songwriter the working songwriters ranked first, Tom Waits, was left off entirely. The argument went nowhere, because nothing sat underneath it. No way to look it up. So I built one.

The book rests on one measurement: 164 songwriters scored across eight measures of songcraft — who built the form, not who got famous singing inside it.

Then that score is set against what the institutions already decided: the Grammys, the Halls of Fame, the magazine lists, added into a single number. The distance between the two is the gap, and the gap is the finding. Roughly half the book moves more than thirty places between what someone built and the credit they were given.

Take Thomas Dorsey, who wrote "Take My Hand, Precious Lord" and effectively invented gospel — the language soul, R&B, and rock all grew out of. Add up every honor the institutions ever gave him and he lands 144th of 164. Measure what he actually built and he lands 30th. A gap of 114 places — the largest in the book. They let him in the door and stood him against the back wall of the room he built.

And it isn't one machine's opinion. Run 226 billion plays by people who never heard of me — the largest record of what listeners actually do — and it draws the same map, putting the right writers side by side without being told the history. Two measurements pointing one way, and a mountain of raw behavior pointing the same way.

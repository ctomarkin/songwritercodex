The Question This Book Answers

Who really built American music?

Not who got famous singing it. Who built it. This book scores 164 songwriters on the craft itself, then sets that against the credit the Grammys and Halls of Fame handed out. The distance between the two is the whole story.

The Fight This Book Settles

In May 2026, the New York Times named the thirty greatest living American songwriters. More than 250 music professionals voted — and then five critics overruled them and picked the names themselves. The songwriter the working songwriters ranked first, Tom Waits, was left off entirely. The argument went nowhere, because nothing sat underneath it. No way to look it up. So I built one.

The book rests on one measurement: 164 songwriters scored across eight measures of songcraft — the lyric, the melody, the structure, how far a writer's work traveled into other hands. It never counts who is famous. It only weighs what was built.

Then that score is set against what the institutions already decided: the Grammys, the Halls of Fame, the magazine lists, added into a single number. The distance between the two is the gap, and the gap is the finding. Roughly half the book moves more than thirty places between what someone built and the credit they were given.

Take Thomas Dorsey, who wrote "Take My Hand, Precious Lord" and effectively invented gospel — the language soul, R&B, and rock all grew out of. Add up every honor the institutions ever gave him and he lands 144th of 164. Measure what he actually built and he lands 30th. A gap of 114 places — the largest in the book. They let him in the door and stood him against the back wall of the room he built.

Then there is a third witness, one I did not build and could not have rigged: 226 billion plays by people who never heard of me. The score is not made of those plays — an old gospel record barely gets streamed at all, and the math knows it. What the listening data gives instead is a map: line up who gets played next to whom, and it sets Chuck Berry beside Fats Domino, lays the hip-hop bloodline out in order, and finds the same forgotten builders the famous lists miss — all without being told a word of the history. My measurement drew one map of this music. Millions of strangers, just listening, drew the same one.

Dorsey is the sharpest proof of all. The listening data can't find him — his songs play constantly, but always under the name of whoever sang them, never his. That silence is not a hole in the method. It is the thing the book is about: build the whole room, hand the songs to other people, and the credit goes to the voices while your name disappears into the walls.
